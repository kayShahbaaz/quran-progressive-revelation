# Surah Al-Qalam (68)

**[2] Surah Al-Qalam (68) | Makkī | 52 Āyāt | Prophetic Year 1–2 (~610–611 CE) | Early Makkan Period**

## Where it sits in the revelation

Al-Qalam is usually placed second or third in the order of revelation — scholars differ slightly on whether it or Al-Muddaththir came first, and both positions are well-supported, neither fringe. What gives this Surah its weight is timing: right after the command to read came the defence of the Prophet's ﷺ character itself — *"you are of a great moral character"* — Allah's direct answer to the earliest slanders against him. No exact month is recorded, just that it belongs to that first year or two, before the community had any real shape.

## What was happening

After the overwhelming experience in the cave, revelation paused for a while — a silence that scholars say genuinely grieved the Prophet ﷺ. Then it resumed. At this point the dawah was still quiet and private. The believers were a handful: Khadijah علیہا السلام, young Ali عليه السلام, Zayd ibn Harithah رضي الله عنه, and Abu Bakr رضي الله عنه. No power, no platform, just a small group gathering to pray.

But word was spreading in Makkah, and the response wasn't yet violence — it was mockery. The label they reached for was *majnun* — mad, possessed — a calculated move, because in that culture, calling someone mad meant people would dismiss everything they said without listening. This is the wound Al-Qalam opens by addressing head-on: *"You are not, by the favour of your Lord, a madman."* Through all of it, the Prophet ﷺ didn't retaliate — he stayed steady, and that steadiness, his character itself, becomes the very thing Allah holds up as proof in this Surah.

## Āyāt 1–7 — the defence

The Surah opens with the mysterious letter *Nun*, then an oath by the pen and what it writes — the same pen Allah claimed credit for teaching in Al-ʿAlaq, now sworn by directly. Then comes the flat denial: you are not mad. Not argued, just stated, by the One who actually knows. Straight after that comes a promise of a reward that never runs out and never comes with strings attached, and then one of the most striking lines in the whole Qur'ān — *"and indeed, you are of a truly magnificent character"* — the same word used elsewhere for Allah's Throne and the Day of Judgment, here applied to a human being's inner character. Sayyidah Aisha رضي الله عنها was once asked to describe the Prophet's ﷺ character and said simply: his character is the Qur'ān.

From there the Surah turns outward — you will see, and they will see, who's really the afflicted one — and settles the matter: only Allah truly knows who is guided and who has strayed. So the instruction follows naturally: don't obey the deniers.

## Āyāt 8–16 — what they actually wanted, and the portrait of a man

It turns out the Quraysh weren't asking for honest debate. They wanted the Prophet ﷺ to soften his message — they'd ease off if he eased up. Allah's response is to refuse that trade outright, and then sketch a portrait of exactly the kind of person to be wary of: someone who swears constantly and means none of it, who wounds people with his tongue and carries gossip between them, who blocks good wherever he can, who's coarse underneath all his social polish, and whose claimed status doesn't even hold up. This is widely connected to figures like Al-Waleed ibn al-Mugheerah, who used his wealth and standing specifically to keep others from accepting Islam.

The why comes next, and it's almost disarmingly simple: because he had wealth and sons, and mistook that for being beyond accountability. When the Qur'ān was recited to him, he waved it off as old fables. The response to that dismissal is sharp — he'll be branded on the very feature he built his pride around.

## Āyāt 17–33 — the parable of the garden

This is the heart of the Surah, and it's worth sitting with as a story. A group of brothers inherited a garden from their father, who'd always set aside a share of the harvest for the poor. The sons quietly decided to end that. They swore in secret they'd harvest it all at dawn, before anyone could come asking — and not one of them said *inshā'Allah*. That small, missing phrase is the whole hinge of what follows: a plan made with no acknowledgment that anything was actually in Allah's hands.

While they slept, something swept through the garden and finished it completely. By morning it looked like a field already stripped bare — and the word used for that is the very same word for the cutting they'd planned to do themselves. They woke up, called to each other, set off whispering their plan to keep every poor person out that day — and arrived to find nothing. Their first reaction was confusion — we must be lost, this can't be the place — before it sharpened into something more honest: we're the ones who've been deprived. The very thing they'd planned for the poor.

One brother among them, described as the most level-headed, had warned them — why didn't you say *SubḥānAllāh*? — and now, standing in the ruin, they finally say it. *"Glory be to our Lord — we were wrongdoers."* Then, very humanly, they turn and start blaming each other before the grief deepens further: *"Woe to us — we were truly transgressors."* And the parable doesn't end in despair. It ends with them turning toward hope — *"perhaps our Lord will give us something better than this — truly we turn to Him with longing."* The Qur'ān never tells us if the garden was restored. That's the point. The turning back mattered more than the outcome.

Then the lesson is drawn out plainly for the Quraysh themselves: this is what punishment looks like, and the punishment of the next life is worse still — if only they knew. Against that, the God-conscious are given gardens that can't be touched by any single night. And then a question that needs no real answer: would Allah really treat those who submit to Him the same as those who reject Him? What is wrong with you, how are you judging this?

## Āyāt 37–47 — dismantling their confidence

This stretch is a chain of pointed questions, each one stripping away a possible basis for the Quraysh's certainty. Do you have a scripture that backs this up? Does it promise you whatever you want? Do you have a binding oath from Allah Himself, lasting to the Day of Judgment? Then go on, ask them which one of them will personally vouch for that. None of them can. The Surah also clears away two other excuses — it's not about money, since the Prophet ﷺ never asked anyone for payment, and it's not about secret knowledge of the unseen, since they have none of that either.

And then comes one of the most sobering ideas in the whole Surah: leave the deniers to Me, Allah says — I will draw them on, step by step, from a direction they won't see coming. Not punishment now, but ease and time given that quietly tightens around them while they mistake it for security. *"My plan is firm."*

## Āyāt 48–50 — a word about patience

Here the Surah turns personal. Be patient, but not like the companion of the whale — a reference to Yunus عليه السلام, who left his people without permission and ended up in genuine distress. Had grace not reached him in time, he would have come out blamed and exposed. Instead, his Lord chose him and placed him among the righteous. It's a gentle warning more than a rebuke — patience that holds on Allah's terms, not patience that snaps and acts alone.

## Āyāt 51–52 — the close

The Surah ends back where the trouble started — people still trying to unsettle the Prophet ﷺ with hostile stares the moment the Qur'ān is recited, still calling him possessed. And the final word answers all of it at once, quietly: *"it is nothing but a reminder for all the worlds."* Not a defence anymore, not an argument — just what it always was, for everyone, for all time.
