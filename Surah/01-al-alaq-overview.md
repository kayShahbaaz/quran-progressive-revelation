# Surah Al-ʿAlaq (96)

**[1] Surah Al-ʿAlaq (96) | Makkī | 19 Āyāt | Prophetic Year 1 (~610 CE) | The Beloved Prophet ﷺ is 40**

## Where it sits in the revelation

The opening five āyāt of Al-ʿAlaq are, by near-universal scholarly agreement (Ibn Hajar, al-Nawawi, and Ibn Kathir all affirm this), the very first words of the Qurʼān revealed to the Beloved Prophet ﷺ. The rest of the Surah (āyāt 6–19) is treated by most scholars as a later addition, revealed in response to Abu Jahl's opposition — so this one Surah actually spans two separate moments of revelation while staying one unified Surah. And before anything else — before prayer, fasting, or charity — the very first thing Allah commanded was to *read*.

## The night it happened

Before getting into the āyāt, it's worth sitting with what was actually going on. Makkah was a city built around trade and tribal pride, its Kaʿbah — built originally by Ibrahim عليه السلام — filled with 360 idols that the Quraysh profited from. It was a harsh place for the weak: women had no standing, and infant daughters were sometimes buried alive.

The Beloved Prophet ﷺ was already known across Makkah as *Al-Amin*, the Trustworthy — even his enemies called him that. He'd been married to Sayyidah Khadijah علیہا السلام for around fifteen years by then, and in the years leading up to revelation he'd taken to retreating alone to the Cave of Hira on Jabal al-Nur, troubled by the state of the world around him. He never once prostrated to an idol.

Then it happened. Jibreel عليه السلام came to him in the cave and embraced him tightly, three times, each time saying *Iqraʼ* — "Read." Each time the Prophet ﷺ answered, *Mā anā bi-qāriʼ* — "I cannot read." Then the first five āyāt came down.

He came down the mountain shaking and went straight to Khadijah علیہا السلام, asking her to cover him. She held him, calmed him, and said words that are still some of the most beautiful lines in the Seerah — that Allah would never disgrace him, because of who he already was: someone who kept family ties, spoke the truth, helped the poor, and stood by people in hardship. She then took him to her cousin Waraqah ibn Nawfal, an old Christian scholar, who recognized what had happened immediately — this was the same angel sent to Musa عليه السلام — and warned, gently, that his own people would turn against him for it.

## Āyāt 1–5 — the first revelation

These five āyāt work as one complete thought about knowledge: where it comes from, how it's passed on, and what it's for. *Iqraʼ* — read — but immediately tied to its source: "in the name of your Lord." Then comes a deliberate humbling: you came from an *ʿalaq*, a clinging clot, utterly dependent. The command to read is repeated, this time alongside the name *Al-Akram* — the Most Generous — because the capacity to know anything at all was never earned, it was given. The pen is named next, as the means by which knowledge survives across generations rather than dying with each person. And the unit closes with the widest possible claim: Allah taught man what he had no way of reaching on his own — all of it, not some of it.

## Āyāt 6–8 — what goes wrong, and the fix

Three āyāt, tightly bound together. After establishing that everything — knowledge, existence, civilisation — is a gift, the very next word is *kallā*, a sharp "no." Man transgresses. And what causes it isn't ignorance, it's *istighnā* — the felt sense of being self-sufficient, of having arrived, of not needing anyone, including God. That feeling can come from wealth, status, even knowledge itself, which is exactly why it's dangerous: it doesn't feel like a problem, it feels like success. The cure comes in seven words: to your Lord is the return. Nothing that made you feel independent travels with you.

## Āyāt 9–14 — the one who forbids

This whole section sits against a real, recorded event: Abu Jahl publicly threatening to crush the Prophet's ﷺ neck if he saw him praying again. The scene opens with someone — unnamed at first — forbidding "a slave when he prays." The word used for the Prophet ﷺ here is *ʿabd*, slave, one of the most honoured titles in the Qurʼān, given to him at his most publicly vulnerable moment. Then comes a run of pointed questions: what if he's upon guidance? What if he's calling people to righteousness? What if, instead, it's the other one who denies the truth and turns away? The contrast is laid out plainly, question by question, until it lands on the line the whole section has been building toward — does he not know that Allah sees? Not just Abu Jahl. Anyone, in any private moment.

## Āyāt 15–19 — the warning, and the final word

A direct warning follows: if he doesn't stop, he'll be seized by the forelock — the front of the head, tied symbolically to defeat, and, separately, to the very part of the brain involved in decision-making. That forelock is then called "lying, sinning" — not describing what the person does so much as what they've become through repetition. Then the contrast sharpens: Abu Jahl is told to call his council of allies; Allah says He'll call the *Zabāniyah*, the angels of Hell — same verb, used for both, which makes the gap in power impossible to miss. It's recorded that when Abu Jahl actually tried to follow through on his threat later, he froze, describing a wall of fire between himself and the Prophet ﷺ.

And then the Surah turns away from Abu Jahl entirely and speaks straight to the Prophet ﷺ, and through him to everyone who comes after: don't obey him — prostrate, and keep drawing near. This is the third and final *kallā* in the Surah, each one marking a turning point. The āyah carries an obligatory prostration on recitation. And the whole Surah's arc comes full circle here — it opened with *Iqraʼ*, read, and closes with *Usjud*, prostrate. Knowledge and submission, together, as one complete thing.
