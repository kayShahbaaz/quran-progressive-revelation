# Surah Al-Muddaththir (74)

**[4] Surah Al-Muddaththir (74) | Makkī | 56 Āyāt | Prophetic Year 1–2 (~610–611 CE) | Early Makkan Period**

## Where it sits, and why it matters

A well-known narration from Jabir ibn Abdullah رضي الله عنه, recorded by al-Bukhārī, places this Surah as the first to be revealed after the *fatrah* — the agonizing pause in revelation following Al-ʿAlaq. That's led some scholars to actually rank it second overall, right after Al-ʿAlaq, though most place it fourth, after Al-Qalam and Al-Muzzammil. Either way, its opening command — "arise and warn" — marks something real: the shift from private prophethood to public mission. Everything before this was interior. This is where it goes outward.

## What was happening

The silence had broken. Jabir رضي الله عنه narrates that the Prophet ﷺ described seeing Jibreel عليه السلام seated on a throne between heaven and earth — a sight so overwhelming he came home trembling, asking again to be covered. And in that moment, this Surah descended: "O you wrapped in garments — arise and warn." Where Al-ʿAlaq gave him prophethood, this gave him the mission itself.

This is also where the Surah connects to a real historical episode. With the pilgrimage season approaching and tribes about to flood into Makkah, the Quraysh elders gathered to settle on a unified story to discredit the Prophet ﷺ before pilgrims could hear him and be moved. They turned to Al-Walid ibn al-Mughirah — wealthy, senior, his opinion carrying enormous weight. He rejected calling him a soothsayer, a poet, a madman — he knew what those actually sounded like, and this wasn't any of them. He admitted that privately. Then, after deliberating, he settled on "sorcery" anyway, purely because it was the most effective lie for keeping people away. Much of this Surah is built directly around that moment.

## Āyāt 1–7 — being sent out, and what it requires of him

The Surah opens almost the same way Al-Muzzammil did — "O you who covers himself" — but the scholars note a specific occasion this time: the Prophet ﷺ had just encountered Al-Walid and been deeply affected by what was said, and came home and wrapped himself again. Allah meets him there, and this time the command faces outward: arise, and warn. Two words, and the whole public mission is contained in them.

What follows are essentially the qualities required of the one carrying that mission — glorify your Lord, keep your garments pure (both literally and as a description of character), decisively leave behind anything filthy or corrupt, don't give generosity while secretly expecting something back in return, and be patient — patient specifically for your Lord's sake, which is what holds everything else in place under pressure.

## Āyāt 8–10 — the Day the warning points to

Then comes the trumpet — the one whose sound marks the Day of Judgment — described in just two words as a difficult day, and a third āyah adds: especially difficult, without ease, for those who deny.

## Āyāt 11–29 — the portrait of one man, and what awaits him

This is the heart of the Surah, and it's framed as Allah taking direct ownership of one specific case — widely understood to be Al-Walid. He's described first by his origin: created alone, with nothing. Then comes everything he was given — wealth spread wide, sons gathered around him as living witnesses to his standing, a life smoothed of obstacles. And then the turn, in one devastating word: yet he wanted more. Not gratitude. More.

The response is sharp: no — he has been knowingly, deliberately obstinate toward the signs, not out of confusion but full awareness. What's coming for him isn't more giving but a punishing, relentless climb. Then the Surah slows down and records, almost frame by frame, exactly how he arrived at his public rejection — he sat and pondered, he calculated, he looked around to gauge the moment, his face moved through a frown into a deeper scowl, he turned his back, let arrogance settle in — and only then did he speak: this is nothing but borrowed magic, nothing but the words of an ordinary man. Two condemnations follow, each opening with "may he be destroyed," the repetition itself making clear that the weight of what he did needed saying twice.

The verdict lands in five words: I will drive him into Saqar. What follows is one of the most vivid descriptions of Hell anywhere in the Qur'ān — it spares nothing, releases nothing, and it scorches the flesh specifically. Then a number arrives almost out of nowhere: nineteen. Just the number, no explanation yet. The next āyah explains it at length — these are angels guarding Saqar, and the number itself was made a test: it would confirm what the People of the Book already had in their own traditions, deepen the faith of believers, and provoke open mockery from those with sickness in their hearts. Recorded history says Abu Jahl actually boasted that ten men could easily handle two each — a boast that exposed exactly the kind of heart the test was designed to reveal.

## Āyāt 30–37 — an oath, an affirmation, and a choice

A series of oaths follows — by the moon, by the departing night, by the brightening morning — building toward one affirmation: Saqar is truly among the greatest of realities, a warning for all humanity. And then comes one of the most respectful lines in the whole Surah: whoever wills may step forward, or whoever wills may hold back. The choice is named plainly and left entirely open.

## Āyāt 38–48 — every soul held by what it earned

The principle is stated with total clarity: every soul is held by what it has earned. Except — and the exception matters — the companions of the right, shown afterward sitting at ease in gardens, talking among themselves, asking about the very people now in Saqar: what brought you here? The answer comes straight from the mouths of those in the fire, four causes named one after another — we did not pray, we did not feed the poor, we used to join in with those who indulged in falsehood, and we denied the Day of Judgment — until certainty itself, meaning death, finally reached us. And by then, the Surah states plainly, no intercession could reach them anymore.

## Āyāt 49–53 — donkeys, a lion, and the real reason

A genuinely searching question follows: so what's actually wrong with them, that they keep turning from the reminder? The answer comes as one of the starkest images in the Qur'ān — like donkeys bolting in panic from a lion. Not reasoning, not weighing anything, just blind flight from the very thing that was sent to help them. Underneath that flight, the Surah says, is a specific demand some of them made: that each person wanted their own personal scroll handed down just to them. The response cuts through it immediately — that's not really the issue. The real reason is that they don't fear the Hereafter. No amount of personalized proof would fix that, because the problem was never a lack of evidence.

## Āyāt 54–56 — the reminder, and the will behind it

The Surah closes, almost word for word like Al-Muzzammil before it, with the same settling line: this is nothing but a reminder. So whoever wills, let him take it to heart. And then the final āyah holds both truths together without letting either one cancel the other — people remember only because Allah wills it, and He alone is worthy of being held in mind with care, and He alone is worthy of forgiving completely. After everything — the warning, Saqar described in full, Al-Walid's portrait, the self-accounting of those already there — the Surah ends resting on exactly those two names: the One worthy of mindfulness, and the One worthy of forgiveness.
